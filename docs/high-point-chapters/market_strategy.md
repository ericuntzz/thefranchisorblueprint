# High Point precedent — Market Strategy & Positioning

> Source: HP_Utah_Market_Strategy_Report.docx (full document), LMF-Franchise Development Strategy and 12-Month Gantt Chart.docx (cross-cutting development sequencing template — partially filled)

## What a strong Market Strategy & Positioning chapter looks like

A category-and-geography chapter — typically 8–14 pages — that defines where the franchisor will sell franchises first, why those markets, and how the brand differentiates against the competitive set in each market. The strongest version pairs a brand snapshot (current footprint, current AUV estimates, operational model) with a competitive analysis, target-market demographics, recommended positioning, recommended fee structure, and a phased go-to-market sequence. The audience is dual: leadership making sequencing decisions and franchisee candidates understanding the long-term plan.

## Structure observed

- Executive summary stating the strategic thesis: how this brand transitions from successful multi-unit operator to scalable franchise
- Brand snapshot: current footprint (number of units, locations), current unit economics with explicit "internal estimate / not yet audited" caveat, operational model description, product offering, customer engagement model, key differentiator (in this case: micro small-batch roasting)
- Competitive concepts and business models: competitor positioning table covering 5–7 named direct competitors, with columns for core business model and strategic positioning
- Competitor gaps and market opportunities: 3–4 named gaps the brand exploits (over-optimization for speed, beverage-only limitations, energy-drink fatigue, roasting quality gap)
- Benchmark franchise economics table: franchise fee, est. initial investment range, royalty / ad fee, public sales metrics for each named competitor (with sources cited)
- Target market entry strategy: why this geography, with concrete numbers (population growth %, median household income, commute-time fit, regional category spend)
- Local market demand drivers: industry data (NCA coffee consumption %, IBISWorld category size & CAGR), regional consumption trends
- Local competitive map: trade-area types (urban dense / family suburban / college-young-pro / peripheral growth) with positioning recommendation for each
- Recommended positioning: how to lean into local authenticity vs. competitor archetypes
- Recommended prototype strategy: 2 named prototypes (flagship café+drive-thru / express drive-thru) sized differently for different trade-area types
- Recommended franchise economics guardrails: initial fee range, royalty %, ad fund %, target initial investment range — each with rationale tied to the competitive set
- Priority site-selection criteria (synthesized with the territory chapter)
- Go-to-market / franchise launch sequencing: phased plan with named timeframes and named geographies
  - Phase 1 (Months 1–4): finalize FDD, audit financials, optimize supply chain
  - Phase 2 (Months 4–12): target X regional markets for first 3–5 franchise units
  - Phase 3 (Months 12–36): expand across the broader region and into adjacent states
- Strategic recommendations: 3–4 must-do items the franchisor cannot skip (protect the differentiator, formalize Item 19, refine multi-daypart menu)
- Risks, watchouts, and key assumptions: 4–5 named risks with mitigation per risk (real-estate availability, labor complexity, cannibalization risk, FDD disclosure readiness)
- Sources & methodology appendix citing every data source

## Depth indicators

- Brand snapshot includes specific AUV ranges with explicit "internal management figure / not audited for FDD Item 19" caveat — protects against earnings-claim exposure.
- Differentiator is named in concrete process terms (micro small-batch roasting → eliminates chemical burn taste) — testable, not vague.
- Competitor table includes 5–7 directly comparable concepts, not aspirational peers — matches operating model and category.
- Benchmark fee table cites public sources (FDDs, brand websites) for every comparable.
- Demographic data uses real numbers with named sources (Census 2024, NCA 2025, IBISWorld 2025).
- Recommended royalty rate (e.g. 5.5%) is positioned against the named competitive set — "matches X, sits below the 6% category ceiling."
- Recommended initial investment range is engineered to land below a category sticker-shock threshold ("below $1M+ freestanding builds").
- Risks section includes the FDD-readiness risk explicitly: unaudited internal AUV figures cannot be marketed; franchisee recruitment should not begin until Item 19 is legally approved.
- Cannibalization risk has a named mitigation: protected territory radii of at least 1.5 miles around existing units.
- Phased launch sequencing names specific submarkets (e.g. Sandy, South Jordan, Lehi as first targets).

## Tone & voice

Strategy-deck register, but written in prose. Calm, numerate, source-cited. The voice anticipates skepticism from both legal counsel and prospective franchisees — names risks in plain language with named mitigations. Disclaimers are present but never inflated. Treats the chapter as a planning document, not a marketing piece.

## What to avoid

- Generic "we will dominate the market" claims with no benchmark, no data, no phased plan.
- AUV figures cited without flagging audit status — creates Item 19 exposure.
- Competitive analysis with 1–2 named competitors instead of the full set — leaves gaps.
- Positioning that boils down to "we have great service" — every competitor claims that.
- Geographic plans that name a state rather than specific submarkets — sequencing matters.
- Skipping the recommended-fee rationale — franchise economics need to be defended against the competitive set, not asserted.
- Mixing FDD disclosure into the strategy chapter as if it's already done — flag it as a phase-1 deliverable.
