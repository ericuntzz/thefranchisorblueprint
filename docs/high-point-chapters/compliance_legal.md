# High Point precedent — FDD Posture & State Strategy

> Source: LMF_FDD_Guide.docx (23-Item FDD breakdown), HP_Document_Review_Franchise_Audit.docx (franchise-readiness review of operational documents), HP_Franchise_Operations_Manual_v3_0.docx (Section 8 Legal, Compliance & Franchisee Obligations)

## What a strong FDD Posture & State Strategy chapter looks like

A two-layer chapter — typically 8–12 pages — that covers (1) how the franchisor's FDD is structured Item by Item, and (2) which states the franchisor will register in, in what order, and on what timeline. The audience is dual: the franchisor's leadership team running the program, and the prospective franchisee evaluating the disclosure document's quality. The strongest version reads like an internal compliance plan that the franchisee is allowed to see.

## Structure observed

- "What is an FDD?" plain-English summary covering FTC Franchise Rule (16 CFR Part 436), NASAA's role, and the 14-day rule with violation consequences
- FDD vs. Franchise Agreement comparison table (purpose, when given, governance, negotiability)
- Registration-state map: every U.S. state classified as Registration / Business Opportunity / Federal Rule, with annual-renewal flags
- FDD development timeline: 8–10 month plan from attorney engagement to first sale, broken into named phases
- 23 Items — one short section per Item with: plain-English summary, what the FTC requires, sample structure where applicable (e.g. Item 6 fee table), complexity rating, and estimated attorney hours
- Item 11 emphasized as the "value proposition" item — most consequential for franchisee marketing
- Item 19 emphasized as optional but high-leverage; ranges, sample sizes, and footnotes are essential
- Item 20 emphasized as the system-health signal — closures, terminations, transfers
- Item 21 audit-financials requirement and the new-franchisor option (audited opening balance sheet)
- Item 23 receipt mechanics that start the 14-day clock
- Master preparation checklist mapping Items to owners (Client / Attorney / Consultant / CPA / Ops Team)
- Red-Flag Radar table: what sophisticated buyers and their counsel scrutinize (litigation patterns, earnings-claim substantiation, closure rates, going-concern qualifications, termination triggers)
- Glossary of key terms (Area Representative, Discovery Day, Master Franchise, Royalty, Validation, etc.)

## Depth indicators

- 14-day rule called out as critical with explicit violation consequences (voidable agreement, FTC penalties, state sanctions).
- Registration-state map distinguishes Registration / Business Opportunity / Federal Rule — three categories, not two.
- 23 Items each have a complexity rating and attorney-hour estimate — gives the franchisor a real budget signal.
- Item 6 includes a sample fee table with columns (Type / Amount / Due Date / Remarks).
- Item 19 framing is conservative: "optional but if any earnings claim is made anywhere — written or oral — it must be supported here," with reasonable-factual-basis requirement.
- Item 17 emphasizes the standardized 23-category termination/renewal/transfer table.
- Master checklist assigns owners by role, not by name.
- Red-Flag Radar names specific patterns (cherry-picked Item 19 samples, going-concern qualifications) that experienced franchise counsel will catch.
- Companion operational-document audit emphasizes that operational manuals must be free of *other-brand* names, location-specific contacts, and store-specific details before any external use — a real failure mode caught in the High Point bundle.
- Operations Manual Section 8 cross-references the FA on every legal point: term length, renewal, territory, transfer, termination grounds, non-compete — and consistently names the FA as governing.

## Tone & voice

Bilingual: "plain English" annotations sit alongside the FTC-required disclosures. The chapter assumes the reader is not a lawyer but will be working with one. Heavy use of tables, complexity ratings, and time estimates. Disclaimer language ("not legal advice; engage qualified franchise counsel") appears repeatedly.

## What to avoid

- Treating the FDD as a single deliverable — it's 23 distinct items with very different complexity profiles. Each needs its own treatment.
- Skipping the Registration vs. Business Opportunity vs. Federal-Rule classification — the timeline and budget swing significantly based on which states the franchisor targets first.
- Single-state strategy framed as "we'll figure it out later" — sequencing matters because Registration States review FDDs before any sales are authorized.
- Item 19 pep talks. Item 19 is risky if poorly substantiated; the chapter should *encourage* franchisors with audited data and *discourage* franchisors without it.
- Operational documents that leak the wrong brand name, store-specific contacts, or corporate-only system references into franchisee-facing materials. The audit document found multiple such failures and treated them as critical fixes — a useful negative example.
