# High Point precedent — Concept & Story

> Source: HP_Franchise_Operations_Manual_v3_0.docx (Section 1 + founder letter), HP_Employee_Handbook_v2.docx (Welcome + Section 1), HP_Utah_Market_Strategy_Report.docx (Brand Snapshot)

## What a strong Concept & Story looks like

A short, confident chapter — typically 1.5–3 pages — that orients the reader to who the brand is, what it stands for, and why a franchisee should attach their name to it. It opens with a founder voice, names the operating model in plain terms, and ends with mission/vision/values that are short enough to be memorized. The reader should finish able to answer "what business am I in?" in two sentences.

## Structure observed

- Founder welcome letter (signed, first-person, addressed to "you")
- One-paragraph origin story explaining the name and the founding belief
- Operating-model sentence describing format(s) (e.g. café + drive-thru hybrid)
- Mission / Vision / Core Values block — each a single line
- A concise "what makes us different" section: 3–5 bullets naming concrete differentiators (proprietary process, daypart range, third-place philosophy, etc.)
- Optional: "what's new in this version" note that tells operators what changed since prior version

## Depth indicators

- Founder letter is signed by a named team and reads like a real human wrote it (warmth, not legal boilerplate).
- Origin story explains the *name* — not just the company history.
- Mission/Vision/Values are short enough to fit on a card and avoid jargon ("create meaningful moments" beats "deliver synergistic experiences").
- Core values list is exactly 5 items, single words.
- Differentiators are concrete and contestable — e.g. "micro small-batch roasting" vs. generic "quality coffee."
- Brand voice is named explicitly (e.g. "knowledgeable but not pretentious; warm but not over-familiar").
- Confidentiality / IP framing appears in the footer ("For Authorized Franchisees Only").

## Tone & voice

Warm, declarative, owner-to-owner. Uses "we" and "you" — never third-person abstraction. Confident without being grandiose. Short sentences. The register is closer to a letter than a brochure. No exclamation points, no marketing puffery, no claims of being "the best."

## What to avoid

- Mission/Vision statements full of consultant-speak verbs ("synergize," "leverage," "empower").
- Origin stories that rehash company milestones without explaining the *why*.
- Differentiator bullets that any competitor could also claim ("great service," "premium quality").
