# High Point precedent — Who You Want as a Franchisee

> Source: HP_Franchisee_Profile_Scoring_Matrix.docx (full document)

## What a strong Who You Want as a Franchisee section looks like

A working evaluation framework — typically 5–8 pages — that turns "we know it when we see it" into a defensible, repeatable scoring system. The section answers four questions: who you want, who you don't want, how you score them, and how the sales process gates them. The strongest version reads like an internal sales-ops document, not a marketing piece — it's written for the franchisor team running the funnel, not for the candidate.

## Structure observed

- "Why a defined profile matters" framing — brand protection, system success, legal defensibility (anti-discrimination posture), sales-team efficiency
- Three franchisee archetypes named explicitly (Owner-Operator / Semi-Absentee / Multi-Unit Developer), with a comparison table covering time commitment, financial requirement, management experience, role, and success factor for each
- Financial qualification floor: minimum credit score (with rationale tied to SBA standards), accepted sources of capital, required runway in months
- Required documentation by stage (CRI / PFS / bank statements / credit auth) with a column for *when* each is required in the funnel
- Scoring matrix with weighted categories totaling 100 points:
  - Financial Strength (~25 pts)
  - Business & Leadership Experience (~20 pts)
  - Local Market Opportunity (~15 pts)
  - Multi-Unit Potential (~10 pts)
  - (plus other categories to reach 100)
- Each category broken into 3–5 sub-criteria with explicit scoring bands and a "DQ" floor
- A 10-step franchise sales process with stage-gate criteria — what score / document / approver is required to advance to each step
- Pre-qualification call script with 8–10 specific questions
- Red flag table: candidate behaviors mapped to what they signal and the recommended action (DQ / probe / proceed with caution)
- Discovery Day playbook with a sample agenda and explicit handling of YES / CONDITIONAL YES / NO responses
- Final candidate scoring worksheet for internal use

## Depth indicators

- Profile is explicitly *not* one-size: three named archetypes with different success criteria.
- Credit-score floor is justified externally (e.g. SBA 7(a) alignment), not an arbitrary number.
- Capital sources are enumerated and each has a documentation requirement (e.g. gift letter for family gifts, ROBS for 401k).
- Scoring categories are weighted, not equal — financial strength carries the heaviest weight.
- "System Compliance" category measures playbook-follower vs. maverick mindset directly — the highest-weighted leadership trait.
- Stage-gate criteria name the *decision-maker* at each gate (Sales Associate → Sales Manager → Director → VP → CEO).
- Red flag dictionary is behavioral and quote-based ("I want to change the menu") — not vibes.
- Discovery Day script includes language for delivering a soft NO without legal exposure.

## Tone & voice

Internal, direct, slightly clinical. The audience is the franchisor's own development team — language assumes they are running a real funnel and need decision criteria, not encouragement. Treats discrimination liability seriously and codes for it in the structure. Uses the word "qualify" more than "sell."

## What to avoid

- Profile descriptions that boil down to "passionate, hardworking, coachable" — every applicant claims those.
- Scoring matrices where every category is weighted equally (financial strength must carry more weight than fit).
- Skipping the legal-defensibility framing — a profile without it can be challenged as discriminatory.
- "Red flags" that are vague feelings rather than observable behaviors.
- Stage gates with no named decision-maker.
