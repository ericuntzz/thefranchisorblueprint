# High Point precedent — Competitive Landscape

> Source: HP_Competitor_Maps_Appendix_Branded.pptx (image-heavy slide deck — competitor location maps), HP_Utah_Market_Strategy_Report.docx (Competitor Positioning & Model Comparison table, Benchmark Franchise Economics table, Local Competitive Map narrative)

## What a strong Competitive Landscape section looks like

A visual + tabular section — typically 5–10 pages of prose plus a separate map appendix — that names the direct competitors, classifies their operating models, benchmarks their franchise economics, and overlays their footprint geographically against the franchisor's target markets. The strongest version answers three questions: who are the comparables, where do they already exist in our target markets, and how does our concept differ in ways that matter to a franchisee deciding between us and them.

## Structure observed

- Direct-competitor positioning table: rows for each named competitor (5–7 concepts), columns for core business model and strategic positioning (e.g. "ultra-compact drive-thru kiosk" vs. "flexible prototypes with drive-thru" vs. "high-throughput drive-thru only")
- Competitor gaps and market opportunities: prose section identifying where the competitive set has structural blind spots (over-optimization for speed leaving "third place" abandoned; beverage-only limitations in throughput-first concepts; energy-drink fatigue in younger demographics; roasting-quality gap created by centralized commercial roasting)
- Benchmark franchise economics table: franchise fee, est. initial investment range, royalty / ad fee, public sales metrics (system AUV, top-25%/top-60% AUV) per competitor — with column for source attribution (FDDs, official portals)
- Local competitive map narrative: trade-area types in the target market characterized by which competitors dominate them and how to position in each:
  - Urban / dense commuter corridors
  - Family-oriented suburban endcaps
  - College / young-professional submarkets
  - Peripheral growth corridors (first-mover opportunity before national chains saturate)
- Visual companion deck (separate .pptx): location maps showing each competitor's existing footprint overlaid against target submarkets — used as appendix, not embedded prose
- Roasting / sourcing differentiator framed as the head-to-head wedge against centralized national chains
- Note on out-of-state corporate energy vs. local authenticity as a positioning lever

## Depth indicators

- Competitive set includes operating-model archetypes, not just brand names — franchisee can map their concept to an archetype and compare to the right peer group.
- Benchmark economics table sources are cited (FDD summaries, brand websites) — not anecdotal.
- Public sales metrics include system AUV *and* top-quartile AUV — signals that the franchisor knows top-of-the-system numbers can mislead.
- Local map narrative breaks down by trade-area *type*, not by submarket — generalizes across markets.
- Differentiator framing is contestable: "micro small-batch roasting eliminates chemical burn taste from high-volume commercial roasting" — testable, not generic.
- Acknowledges that direct competitors will be aggressively bidding on the same drive-thru pads — names this as a real-estate risk, not a market-share narrative.
- Visual map deck is treated as an appendix to the strategy report, not as the section itself — structure note: image-heavy assets stay in companion decks, prose section cites them.

## Tone & voice

Analyst register. Comparative, source-cited, disciplined. Names peer concepts directly without disparagement. Gives credit where the competitor is genuinely strong (e.g. "Dutch Bros: hyper-friendly culture, long hours, Rebel energy drinks, protein coffee") before identifying the gap. Treats the competitive set as a category, not as enemies.

## What to avoid

- Vague "no real competitors" claims — every category has direct comparables.
- Competitor lists that mix categories (drive-thru kiosk, traditional café, fast-casual restaurant) without classifying by archetype.
- Benchmark numbers without source attribution — invites disputes.
- Positioning that requires the competitor to be bad ("they're slow / unfriendly / overpriced") — the strongest positioning works even if the competitor is excellent.
- Embedded competitor maps inside the prose section — they belong in a companion visual deck so prose stays editable.
- Skipping the head-to-head pad-bidding risk — it's the single biggest constraint on franchise expansion in saturated markets.
